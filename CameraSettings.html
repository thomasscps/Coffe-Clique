Different Modes

    Automatic Mode

  • The camera does all of the work for you without you having to adjust anything.
  • Great for beginners or when you need to take a photo quickly.

    • Aperture Priority Mode

  • You can adjust your F-Stop value while the camera adjusts ISO and Shutter Speed.
  • Best used when you want to just control your depth or field.

    • Shutter Priority Mode

  • You can adjust your shutter speed and the camera adjusts ISO and Aperture for you.
  • Best used when you want to control the speed you are shooting something at.

    • Landscape Mode

  • Uses a narrower aperture so you can capture the largest depth of field.
  • Best used for taking pictures of scenery.

    • Portrait Mode

  • Sets a wide aperture to just make your subject in focus.
  • Best used when taking headshots or photos of a person as your subject.

    • Night Mode

  • Increases your ISO and slows Shutter Speed.
  • Used to capture dimly lit scenes or shooting at night.

    • Manual Mode

  • You have full control of all the settings.
  • Best used for having full control of the image produced. Trade off is that it is more difficult to shoot in this mode.


Manual Settings

    ISO

  • ISO controls how sensitive the camera is to light.
  • The higher the ISO the more sensitive the camera will be to light. Typically want to do this with less lighting but it introduces a lot of noise.
  • The lower the ISO the less sensitive your camera is to light and you get less grain in your image.
  • Typically you see recommendations to keep your ISO to around 100-125 and not raise it to high.

    • F Stop or Aperture

  • Aperture or more commonly referred to as F-Stop is the setting that controls how much light you let through the lens.
  • The more narrow your aperture the less amount of light you let in. You get a sharper background and a deeper depth of field. The higher the F-Stop value the narrower you get.
  • The wider your aperture the more light you let in. You get a shallower depth of field and a blurred background. The lower the F-Stop value the wider the aperature is.

    • Shutter Speed

  • Shutter speed controls how long your shutter stays open allowing light to come in.
  • A slow shutter speed lets in more light and captures a lot of motion blur. A slower speed is anything more than 1 second.
  • A faster shutter speed lets in less light and freezes motion. A faster speed is anything over 1/1000 of a second.
